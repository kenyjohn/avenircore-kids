# AI Prompts for Group Work: 10 Safe, Practical Ideas for the Classroom

The most common mistake teachers make when introducing AI to group work is treating it as a writing tool. Groups ask AI to produce a report — and then submit it with minor edits. The work is done, but nothing has been learned.

The far more valuable use of AI in collaborative settings is as a thinking partner: something that pushes back, offers perspectives the group hasn't considered, and helps them stress-test their own ideas.

## The framework: AI as a thinking partner, not a ghostwriter

Before introducing any AI prompt to a group, establish what you are calling "The Group Rule":

*"AI is a thinking tool, not a writing tool. We use it to challenge and develop our thinking. We never copy-paste. We never submit AI output as our own work."*

## The Prompt Journal

Ask each group to keep a Prompt Journal with three columns:
1. What we asked the AI
2. What the AI said
3. What we decided — and why

The Prompt Journal makes students' thinking visible and makes it obvious whether AI was used as a tool or as a shortcut.

## 10 group work prompts ready to use

### 1. The devil's advocate
*"We are preparing a [debate / presentation / argument] for [topic]. Give us three strong counter-arguments that someone opposing our position would make."*

### 2. The gap finder
*"Here is our proposed solution to [problem]. Identify two logical gaps or weaknesses in our reasoning that we haven't addressed yet."*

### 3. The expert lens
*"Pretend you are a [historian / scientist / economist / urban planner]. From your perspective, what are the three most important questions we should be asking about our project?"*

### 4. The simplification challenge
*"We have gathered this research: [paste notes]. Help us identify the three most important ideas and explain each one in a single sentence that a ten-year-old could understand."*

### 5. The stakeholder map
*"Our project is about [topic]. Identify four different groups of people who would be affected by this and explain what each group's main concern would be."*

### 6. The fact-checker
*"Here are three claims from our research: [list claims]. Are each of these accurate? If any are partially accurate or disputed, explain what the nuance is."*

### 7. The analogy builder
*"We are designing a [product / service / solution] for [audience]. Give us three analogies or metaphors that could help us explain our idea simply to someone who has never heard of it before."*

### 8. The precedent search
*"Has anyone ever tried to solve [problem] before? Give us two or three examples from history or other contexts where a similar challenge was addressed, and briefly explain what worked or didn't."*

### 9. The risk assessment
*"Our proposed solution is [describe solution]. What are the three most likely ways this could go wrong, and what could be done to reduce each risk?"*

### 10. The one-sentence test
*"In one sentence, summarise the most important thing we have learned through this project."*

## Structuring a 45-minute session with AI in the loop

A practical structure that works for classes from Year 7 upward divides the period into three movements. First, ten minutes of offline discussion without any devices: groups clarify their question and agree on what they need from AI. Second, fifteen minutes of guided AI use with the Prompt Journal running in parallel — each query and response is logged. Third, twenty minutes synthesising: students write or present in their own words, explicitly referencing where they agreed or disagreed with the model.

This rhythm prevents the common failure mode where groups spend forty minutes chatting with AI and five minutes pasting output into a slide. The teacher can circulate during the middle phase, spot-checking journals and asking, "What surprised you in what the model said?" That question alone often surfaces genuine thinking.

## Differentiation when groups have mixed prior knowledge

Mixed-ability groups can still use shared AI prompts if the task is layered. Stronger students might take the "expert lens" prompt and push for depth; others might use "simplification challenge" on the same material. The teacher can assign roles within the group — Research Lead, AI Dialogue Lead, Synthesis Lead — so participation does not collapse to whoever is fastest with the keyboard.

Remember that the goal is not equal technical fluency with the tool; it is equitable contribution to the group's intellectual output. A student who struggles with typing might excel at oral synthesis after the group has interacted with AI together.

## Assessment considerations

When students use AI in group work, consider how you will assess their engagement:
- Ask groups to submit their Prompt Journal alongside the final product
- Include a brief in-class reflection where groups explain how AI influenced their thinking
- Consider an oral component where students explain a decision they made differently from what AI suggested

## When collaboration goes off-track

If a group fixates on amusing but irrelevant AI tangents, bring them back with a single concrete question tied to your learning objective: "Which part of our syllabus outcome does this help us address?" If two groups produce eerily similar AI paragraphs, that is a signal to run a short in-class discussion on what "writing in our own words" means for your subject — not necessarily a disciplinary moment, but a calibration.

Documenting one or two of these mid-course corrections in your feedback to students reinforces that AI group work is a skill that improves with practice, just like traditional group work always has been.

## Building trust with parents and administrators

Some stakeholders hear "AI group work" and imagine unsupervised internet browsing. A one-page explainer you can reuse each term — listing approved tools, the Group Rule, and how journals are assessed — turns vague anxiety into something concrete you can defend in a meeting. Emphasise that the final product is still student-authored and that AI appears in the chain of reasoning, not as the author of record.

Offer to run a fifteen-minute showcase evening segment where two volunteer groups walk families through a Prompt Journal page live, with names and personal details scrubbed. Seeing the thinking beats reading the policy.

## Safety checklist before you begin

- Using a district-approved tool with a signed data privacy agreement ✓
- Students using a shared or teacher-managed account, not personal accounts ✓
- No student names or personal information will be input ✓
- Students understand the Group Rule before the session begins ✓
- Each group has a Prompt Journal template ready ✓
