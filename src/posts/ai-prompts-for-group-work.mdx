Using AI as a individual "ghostwriter" is easy. Using it as a **Collaborative Thinking Partner** is a skill that will define the 2026 workforce.

## How to Structure AI Group Work Safely

Before handing a prompt to a group of students, give them "The Group Rule": **Never copy-paste.** The AI is only a "silent member" of the team to spark ideas, not do the work.

### 5 Safe and Effective Group Work Prompts

1.  **The "Devil's Advocate" Prompt**:
    *"We are preparing a debate on [Topic]. Give us 3 strong counter-arguments a person would make against our main point."*
2.  **The "Check My Thinking" Prompt**:
    *"Here is our proposed solution to [Problem]. Point out 2 logical flaws or gaps in our plan that we haven't considered yet."*
3.  **The "Creative Brainstorm" Prompt**:
    *"We are designing a [Product/Service/Story]. Give us 5 'out-of-the-box' metaphors or analogies we could use to explain our idea to a younger audience."*
4.  **The "Expert Persona" Prompt**:
    *"Pretend you are a [Role: e.g. Urban Planner/Biologist/Historian]. Give us 3 questions we should ask about our current project from your perspective."*
5.  **The "Summarize and Simplify" Prompt**:
    *"We have gathered all of this research [Paste Research]. Can you help us find the 3 most important themes and explain them simply for our 1st-grade presentation?"*

## Teacher Best Practice: The "Prompt Journal"
Ask students to keep a "Prompt Journal" where they record:
- What they asked the AI.
- What the AI said.
- **Why they chose to use (or ignore) the AI's advice.**

> "AI is a mirror for our own thinking—it helps us see our own gaps and biases if we use it correctly."

## Teacher FAQ: Is this safe for COPPA/FERPA?
As long as students are not inputting personal data and you are using a district-approved AI platform with a shared group account, these prompts are safe and effective.
